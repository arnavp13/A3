I used census data for this visualization. It gives me three important statistics: the total population, the average level of education, and the average median income for each county within the major cities. My goal was to display possible connections between these factors without making the visual misleading. To do this, I chose a simple scatter plot with two simple axes: the x-axis shows "Avg. Educational Attainment" and the y-axis shows "Avg. Median Income" (in dollars). The labels are very clear, and the scales are smooth from zero on, which keeps the data from looking skewed. Since the dataset covers a lot of different areas, I used color to show the population of each county. There is a the key on the right side that shows how the color scale works and what each point's color means based on its total population. This lets people see how areas with differing populations have different levels of schooling and income without getting in the way of the main plot. The audience can make their own assumptions and connection with population without the graph trying to mislead them. There are no axis breaks or exaggerated scaling used in any of the data transformations. For example, the average median income and educational achievement are found by adding up all the data points. To keep things simple, the chart has a consistent color scheme, a balanced layout of axis tick marks, and no useless features. The scatter map also shows that there isn't a single outlier that stands out or is hidden. Instead, there is a wide range of points. This large amount of data makes sure that the visualization doesn't depend on picking out outliers. Overall, this visualization tries to show the data honestly and correctly by using a simple scatter plot with color-coding for population, as well as clear labels and consistent scales. This is an example of a "white hat" approach that is focused on being honest and easy to understand rather than trying to trick someone.
